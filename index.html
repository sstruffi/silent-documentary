Videography / Silent Portrait

Huether Hotel Portrait

A short silent documentary capturing everyday life at the Huether Hotel in Uptown Waterloo, including the café, lounge, and Jazz Room, and showing how different routines and environments come together in one place.

Silent Documentary Observational Filmmaking Visual Storytelling Editing Director / Shooter / Editor
Watch on YouTube Back to Videography

Project Overview

The film focuses on the Huether Hotel as a place people move through in different ways throughout the day. In the morning, the atmosphere is quieter in the café, with coffee, pastries, and early guests. As the day progresses, the space becomes busier, with people meeting friends, working on laptops, passing through, and spending time in the Jazz Room.

Rather than using spoken explanation, the project tells the story visually through details like coffee, cups placed on tables, people moving through the building, conversations, and live jazz. The goal was to show the hotel as a place where different routines and environments come together while relying entirely on observation, pacing, and visual contrast.

My Role

I was responsible for the creative direction and execution of this project, start to finish. My role included planning the concept, choosing the locations, filming on site, selecting shots that would communicate the atmosphere of the Huether Hotel, and editing the final piece into a clear visual narrative.

I made all final decisions and sequencing so the film could communicate a sense of place without dialogue. I also focused on how each shot connected to the next, making sure the transitions felt natural and that the viewer could understand the rhythm of the space through visuals alone.

What I Was Responsible For

  • Developing the concept for a silent observational portrait
  • Planning what times of day to capture and why they mattered
  • Filming wide, medium, and detail shots on location
  • Capturing footage that showed both atmosphere and routine
  • Editing the final sequence for mood, flow, and clarity
  • Using visual storytelling instead of interviews or narration

Shooting Style

The shooting style is calm and observational, using tripod and hand-held shots. Wide shots show the space and how people move through the café, lounge, and bar. Medium shots focus on activity at the seating areas, café counter, and tables, while close-ups capture details like coffee, hands typing, and cups on tables.

A few slow-moving shots follow customers as they walk to the counter or bring drinks to their seats, but the camera stays subtle and out of the way. Instead of focusing only on faces, many shots frame people from behind, over the shoulder, or through hands and movement to create emotion and tell the story through gesture and environment.

Production Plan

The project was planned for the Huether Hotel and The Jazz Room in Uptown Waterloo. The production plan included filming across different moments of the day so the final piece could show a fuller portrait of the space. I wanted to contrast quieter café moments with busier periods later on, allowing the environment itself to shape the structure of the film.

The planned equipment included a Canon R7, 18-150 mm lens, Canon SX730 HS, and a Manfrotto tripod. This setup gave me flexibility to capture both stable environmental shots and more intimate handheld details without interrupting the natural flow of the location.

Process and Design Thinking

A major part of this project was thinking carefully about hierarchy and visual clarity. Because the film is silent, every shot had to do more work. I had to decide what the viewer needed to see first, what details would build atmosphere, and how to move from one area of the hotel to another without confusing the audience.

I approached the project by first identifying the main story: the Huether Hotel as a shared space with many different uses. From there, I built a shot list around moments that would support that idea, including establishing shots, movement through hallways and seating areas, and smaller details that grounded the viewer in the environment. In editing, I selected clips that strengthened this progression and removed anything that interrupted the pacing or distracted from the atmosphere.

This project strengthened my understanding storytelling. Even in film, the viewer needs a clear path through the work. I focused on making the sequence easy to follow, visually balanced, and emotionally consistent, so the audience could stay oriented while still feeling immersed in the setting.

Evidence of Learning

Through this project, I learned how much intention is needed when telling a story without sound or explanation. I became more aware of how framing, duration, and shot order affect meaning, and I learned to trust subtle details rather than relying on direct explanation.

I also improved my ability to observe a space before filming it. Instead of trying to capture everything, I focused on the moments that best supported the story. That helped me create a more cohesive final piece and strengthened my editing choices.

Impact

The final film presents the Huether Hotel as more than just a building. It becomes a layered social environment shaped by routine, atmosphere, and movement. By showing how different people occupy the same place in different ways, the project creates a portrait that feels calm, human, and grounded in observation.

This piece reflects my interest in documentary and visual storytelling, especially projects that communicate tone and meaning through structure, pacing, and detail. It also shows how I think through a creative problem, from concept to filming to final edit.

Outcome

This project shows my interest in observational film and visual storytelling through mood, pacing, and detail. It focuses on building atmosphere through framing, movement, and editing rather than dialogue, and highlights the type of film work I want to keep exploring in my videography practice.
